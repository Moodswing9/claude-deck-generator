# The Closing Slide & Script
## AI Presentation Generator — Master Closer Framework

*This is the last thing the room sees. It must be the only thing they remember.*

---

## THE SLIDE

**Headline:**
> "Every day you wait, you pay for it."

**Three lines. Nothing else:**

```
$[X]      — what one presentation costs your organization today
60 sec    — what it costs with this tool
[X] days  — until it pays for itself completely
```

**One command at the bottom:**
```
python generate.py "Your next meeting"
```

**Design:** White background. Black numbers. No logo. No decoration.
The numbers are the design.

## THE CLOSING SCRIPT

*(Walk to the front. Do not look at the slide. Look at the room.)*

---

**THE COST OF INACTION:**

"I want to tell you what happens if you leave this room and do nothing.

[BEAT]

Next Monday, someone on your team will open PowerPoint.
They will spend four hours on a deck that needed to exist by Thursday.
They will revise it three times.
They will send a version that's 80% of what it could have been
because time ran out — not ideas.

[BEAT]

That will happen again the following Monday.
And the Monday after that.

[LONG]

You will pay for every one of those Mondays.
Not on an invoice. Not on a dashboard.
In the compounding cost of your best people doing your lowest-leverage work."

---

**THE URGENCY:**

"Here's what I know about this room.

Everyone in it is capable of making this decision today.
The tool is free to start. The setup is three commands.
The first presentation takes sixty seconds.

[BEAT]

The only thing standing between you and never building a deck from scratch again
is whether you open your laptop before you walk out that door.

[LONG]

Every deal I've ever closed came down to one moment —
the moment the room decided the cost of waiting
was higher than the cost of acting.

[BEAT]

We are in that moment right now."

---

**THE SIMPLICITY:**

"I'm not asking for a budget approval.
I'm not asking for a pilot program.
I'm not asking for a meeting with your team.

[BEAT]

I'm asking for sixty seconds of your time
and three lines in a terminal.

[LONG]

That's it. That's the entire ask.
Sixty seconds to find out if I've been telling you the truth
for the last twelve minutes."

---

**THE CALL TO ACTION:**

*(Point to the command on the screen. Don't rush it.)*

"This command is on the screen behind me.
It's in the README. It's in your inbox if you give me thirty seconds after this.

[BEAT]

Type a topic.
Hit enter.
Read what comes back.

[LONG]

If it's not worth your time, delete it and forget this conversation.
If it is —

[HOLD — make eye contact with one person]

you'll know what to do."

*(Sit down. Say nothing else. Do not add 'any questions.' Do not smile and wait.
The silence is the close. Let it work.)*

---

## WHY THIS CLOSE WORKS

**The cost of inaction frame:**
You don't sell urgency by saying "act now." You paint the future of inaction in such specific, vivid detail that the audience sells themselves. Monday. PowerPoint. Four hours. Three revisions. 80% of what it could have been. Every word is a mirror — they've lived this scene. That recognition creates urgency no pitch ever could.

**The simplicity disarm:**
Every "yes" has a hidden "but how hard is it?" objection underneath it. You preemptively destroy it: *no budget, no pilot, no meeting* — just three commands and sixty seconds. The ask becomes so small it's irrational to say no.

**The truth test close:**
"Find out if I've been telling you the truth" reframes the CTA as an experiment, not a commitment. Nobody fears running an experiment. The moment they run it and it works, they've closed themselves.

**The silence:**
Most closers lose the room by filling the silence after the ask. The silence IS the mechanism. The audience's discomfort is them arguing themselves into yes. Every word you add after the close is a tax on that process. Sit down. Let the room do the work.
