# Data Narrative Framework
## McKinsey-Style: Raw Numbers → Boardroom Decisions

*Replace bracketed figures with your actual data before presenting.
Every structure, sentence, and visual here is designed to survive a CFO.*

---

## THE GOVERNING PRINCIPLE

A McKinsey partner never reads data. They prosecute a case with it.
Every number you show must do one of three things:

1. **Indict the status quo** — make the current situation feel unacceptable
2. **Prove the solution works** — make the outcome feel inevitable
3. **Quantify the cost of inaction** — make doing nothing feel irresponsible

If a number doesn't do one of those three things, cut it.

## ACT I — INDICT THE STATUS QUO

### The Numbers to Highlight

| Metric | Your Figure | What It Proves |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Hours spent per presentation | [X] hrs | The problem has a body count |
| Presentations created per month/quarter | [X] | Scale the damage |
| Avg. hourly cost of the person making them | $[X]/hr | Convert time to money |
| Number of revision cycles before approval | [X] | The process is broken, not the people |
| % of slides that make it into final deck unchanged | [X]% | Most of the work is waste |

### The Story They Prove

> "This is not a productivity problem. This is a capital allocation problem.
> Your highest-paid people are doing your lowest-leverage work."

### The One Number to Put on Screen

**Cost per presentation = Hours × Hourly rate × Revision cycles**

Put the math on screen. Say nothing while the room does it in their heads.
When they land on the number, that's when you speak.

### The Visual

**A single equation. No chart. No pie chart. No bar graph.**
White background. Black numbers. One multiplication sign.

> `[X] hours × $[X]/hr × [X] revisions = $[X] per deck`

Boards respond to arithmetic, not infographics.

### The Exact Words to Say

*"This is what one presentation costs your organization.
Not the printing. Not the room. Not the people in it.
Just the act of making the slides.
Multiply that by [X] presentations a quarter.
That's [$X] a year — to move bullet points around a screen."*

[HOLD — let them do the math]

*"That number isn't in your P&L. But it should be."*

## ACT II — PROVE THE SOLUTION WORKS

### The Numbers to Highlight

| Metric | Before | After | Delta |
|--------|--------|-------|-------|
| Time to first draft | [X] hrs | 60 sec | –[X]% |
| Revision cycles to approval | [X] | [X] | –[X] cycles |
| Stakeholder satisfaction score | [X]/10 | [X]/10 | +[X] pts |
| Presentations created per week | [X] | [X] | +[X]× output |
| Time reclaimed per person per month | — | [X] hrs | [X] hrs back |

### The Story They Prove

> "The tool doesn't improve the process. It replaces it.
> These aren't efficiency gains. They're category changes."

### The One Number to Put on Screen

**Time reclaimed per person per year.**

Convert hours to weeks. Boards think in headcount and capacity, not minutes.

> `[X] hours/month × 12 = [X] hours/year = [X] working weeks returned per person`

### The Visual

**A before/after timeline. Two bars. Nothing else.**

- Left bar: time from brief to approved deck, old process
- Right bar: time from brief to approved deck, new process
- No legend. No gridlines. Just the two bars and the gap between them.

Label the gap: *"[X] hours you'll never get back — until now."*

### The Exact Words to Say

*"This bar is your Monday morning.
Four hours. Formatting, revising, starting over.
This bar is your Monday morning with the tool.
Sixty seconds to first draft. [X] minutes to final.
The gap between these two bars — that's not efficiency.
That's [X] hours a month you get to spend on the work that actually moves the needle."*

[BEAT]

*"For a team of [X] people, that's [X] weeks of capacity recovered.
Every quarter. Without a single new hire."*

## ACT III — QUANTIFY THE COST OF INACTION

### The Numbers to Highlight

| Metric | Figure | Frame |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Annual cost of status quo (from Act I) | $[X] | What you're spending |
| Annual cost of the tool | $[X] | What you'd spend |
| ROI | [X]× | What you'd return |
| Payback period | [X] days | How fast it pays |
| Opportunity cost of one wasted senior hour | $[X] | What you're really trading |

### The Story They Prove

> "Every month you don't act, you're choosing to pay $[X]
> for a problem that costs $[X] to solve."

### The One Number to Put on Screen

**Payback period in days.**

Not months. Not quarters. Days. Boards approve things that pay back in days.

> `$[X] tool cost ÷ $[X] monthly savings = [X] days to payback`

### The Visual

**A single number, full screen. No decoration.**

> **[X] days.**

Below it, in smaller text: *"Time to full payback."*

That's the slide. Nothing else.

### The Exact Words to Say

*"I want to show you one number.
[X] days.
That's how long it takes for this tool to pay for itself — completely.
After [X] days, every hour it saves is pure margin.
The question in this room is not whether to do this.
The question is why we haven't done it already."*

[LONG]

*"I'll tell you why. Because the cost of the status quo doesn't show up on a dashboard.
It shows up in the hours your best people aren't spending on your biggest problems.
It shows up in the decks that went out half-finished because time ran out.
It shows up in the deals you didn't close because the pitch wasn't sharp enough."*

[BEAT]

*"This fixes that. In [X] days. Fully paid."*

| Slide | What's on screen | What you say |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| **1** | The cost equation | "This is what one presentation costs." |
| **2** | Annual total | "Multiply by [X] decks a year." |
| **3** | Before/after timeline | "This is the gap." |
| **4** | Capacity recovered | "This is what you get back." |
| **5** | [X] days. | "This is how fast it pays." |
| **6** | One command | "This is how you start." |

Six slides. No transitions. No animations. No decorative elements.
The data is the design.

---

## THE THREE NUMBERS THAT WIN THE ROOM

If the board remembers nothing else, they must walk out with these three:

> **$[X]** — what each presentation costs today
> **[X] hours** — what the tool returns per person per month
> **[X] days** — how fast it pays back

Write them on a whiteboard if you have to.
Say them at the start. Say them at the end.
They are the case. Everything else is evidence.

---

## WHAT TO CUT

| Cut this | Replace with |
|----------|-------------|
| Percentage improvements | Absolute numbers — "$40K saved" beats "32% reduction" |
| Averages | Extremes — the worst case indicts, the best case inspires |
| Year-over-year trends | Point-in-time comparisons — before vs. after is cleaner |
| More than 3 metrics per slide | The one number that matters most |
| Any chart with a legend | A chart simple enough to need no legend |

---

## THE MCKINSEY CLOSE

The last thing you say is not a summary. It is a forcing function.

*"We have two choices.
We spend $[X] a year building slides.
Or we spend $[X] once and spend the rest of the year building the business.
I know which one I'd put in the budget.
The question is whether you agree."*

[HOLD]

Then stop. Do not soften it. Do not add context. Do not smile.
Let the number do its job.
